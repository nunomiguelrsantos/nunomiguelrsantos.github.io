An exploration of my music listening habits between 2016 and 2021

Soundtracking Habits

An Infographics and Visual Storytelling class project | Spring 2022

Last.fm is a tracking tool that allows you to track every song you listen to into a detailed profile account. In this account, you can connect with other users, find new artists recommendations and, the main idea of last.fm, you can find a library of your “scrobbles” - the word used to determine every track played and registered. If such type of account sounds familiar, it is because it might remind you what major streaming services such as Apple Music, Tidal and Spotify are offering now. But Last.fm is not a streaming service. Their popularity peaked in the latter years of the 2000’s when it offered a radio streaming service as well but today works mostly as an online music database. In a time where streaming services are the major source of how people consume music, it still amazes me how this website never regained some of its popularity. It is even more surprising when Spotify has a successful campaign with Spotify Wrapped when it gives you once a year, a glimpse of your minutes spent listening and most-played songs. The idea of showing users their music listening history is appealing and last.fm offers you exactly that. Not only once a year, but readily available on your profile whenever you want. For a big part of the last decade, my usage of this website was close to nothing but because I had my profile connected to my streaming service account, last.fm kept registering all songs I have listened to, digitally.

While researching for data sources to work with, I instantly thought of last.fm. While the website doesn’t give you a direct access, there are some online tools that give you the possibility to download a .csv file when you provide your last.fm username. I first started using Last.fm in 2009 and I used their "audioscrobbler" connected to my computer's media player. Today, I have Last.fm connected to streaming services. So, when I tried to get my data source, a .csv file with around 120.000 entries came out, presenting "scrobbles" from April 2009 to the first months of 2022. While it might be tempting to try and sort out all this data, I am still new in developing visualizations from charts so I decided to narrow the data source from the beginning of 2016 to end of 2021.

64346 scrobbles in 6 years

The first thing that seemed natural to do, was to divide the total amount of scrobbles registered in the last 6 years into a year visualization. I was not surprised to see 2020 as the year with the higher amount of plays, but it did surprised me to see the discrepancy between 2020 and 2016.
scrobbles between 2016 and 2021

Radiohead, Damien Jurado and 3 nights listening to Max Richter.

Exploring a little further, I can examine how the top 6 artists were part of my listening habits during this time. While all of them seemed to have been part of my music of choice in a (more or less) constant way, the artist Max Richter is an exception. Seeing the data displayed in a heatmap, the big majority of the scrobbles of the artist were tracked in just a month: I remember playing the album Sleep through 3 nights in June 2020. An album with 204 tracks.
top 6 artists in every month between 2016 and 2021

The usual suspects + 5332 artists and playlists

When comparing the trend of all top 6 artists and adding the total amount of scrobbles between them, one thing caught my attention: Although 2016 was the year I have listen to less music in this 5 year period, I have listen to all these artists more in 2016 than in 2018.
trend from the top 6 artists between 2016 and 2021

But when I try to compare these 6 artists with all the other tracked artists, I realize there is a huge amount of artists with less scrobbles that accounts for the majority of music listened. After all, these 6 artists together have 4266 scrobbles in a 5 year period with 64346 scrobbles. Just 6,5% of the total amount. I recognized that one of my habits is listening to a lot of playlists. While I cannot search for playlists in this data source, the number of artists tracked, 5338, can only be justified by that habit.
running sum of each artist between 2016 and 2021

Sleep, compilations and yearly favorites

Looking at the most played albums, we again see the already explained expection of Max Richter's Sleep. But now, I discovered one more exception, although not as evident as the previous. Using the tooltip, it is possible to see how many times the most played songs on the album were scrobbled. While most of the albums in this list have my favourite songs being played around 20 and 30 times, the top songs on the R.E.M. compilation of "Complete Rarities 1988-2011" were played only twice. Like Sleep, this album has a huge number of songs, and therefore, despite I might have listen to the songs of this album once or twice, it managed to appear in the top 20.
top 20 albums

Unscheduled commute, morning walks and private sessions

Half surprise here. I do listen to more music in the mornings. While I don't have a listening routine for commute (far from it), I expected a little more plays between 7am and 8am. I did also expected more scrobbles between 8pm and 10pm but the low numbers at this time might be a reflection of when I choose another format (CD's or vinyl) that are not tracked by last.fm.
listening clock - total amount of scrobbles per hour of the day

Weekend Off

Dividing the total amount of scrobbles between the days of the week, it is very interesting to see how constant and persistent my listening habits are during weekdays and how the amount of scrobbles on saturdays and sundays fall to half.
